Competing Theories

What People Believe

Scientists have no confirmed cause. Into that vacuum, survivor stories have bred two dominant theories — and the debate between them has become as contagious as the disease itself.

Theory 01 — Saliva Mutation
The bacteria mutated specifically in dogs' saliva. Only direct oral contact near the eyes can transmit the disease. Casual proximity is safe.
Theory 02 — Full Contact Spread
Any point of bodily contact can transmit the bacteria. There is no safe distance. All physical contact with infected dogs is dangerous.

What Is It

Eye-bies is a fictional disease that creates a profound ethical dilemma: people must decide what kind of relationship they will have with dogs when that relationship costs them their ability to see color. It is speculative — rooted in present-day social tensions around pet ownership, public health, and animal rights.

The Condition

Infected individuals progressively lose color vision — a condition called achromatopsia. The process is painless at first. Colors simply become less saturated over weeks, then months, until the world is entirely gray. There is no known reversal. No known cure.

Origin

The disease emerged from a bacterial mutation in dogs on a remote Atlantic island. The mutation was invisible — no symptoms in the animals, no warning to the humans who lived alongside them. By the time the first cases were understood, the infection had spread across the entire island community.

Status

The island has been quarantined. Survivors who escaped to North America continue to tell their stories. Scientists disagree about the mechanism of transmission. Activists and governments remain at odds. The antidote search continues.

Transmission beyond the island has not been confirmed — but experts warn the window for containment is closing. Scientists remain divided on whether human-to-human spread is possible. Pet Owners

Loyalty Over Sight

Surveys of island survivors consistently show the same pattern: even after understanding the risk, the majority of dog owners chose to keep their animals rather than seek treatment. Many describe the choice as simple — "It's just color. My dog is my family." Public health officials have described this as the central obstacle to containment.

Society

A Community Divided

On the island, neighbors stopped speaking. Dog owners were shunned — or sheltered. Protests broke out over forced quarantine orders. The social fractures of Eye-bies mapped almost perfectly onto existing political divisions: individual rights versus collective safety. Scientists say if the disease reaches the mainland, these divisions will only deepen.

No Consensus

No Agreed Response

Three separate international health bodies have issued conflicting recommendations. One calls for immediate culling of dogs in affected areas. Another argues for quarantine and testing. A third says the data is insufficient for any policy. Meanwhile, no antidote research is funded because no government can agree on the mechanism of spread.

Pathway 01

The Water Bowl Pathway

The leading hypothesis. Bacteria multiply in the water bowls of infected dogs. When a dog drinks, the bacteria colonize the saliva. Face licking — especially near the eyes — deposits the bacteria directly into one of the body's most vulnerable points. Infection follows within 10–14 days. This pathway is supported by 6 of the 9 surviving islanders.

Pathway 02

The Contact Pathway

Two survivors described never allowing their dogs to lick their faces — yet still contracted Eye-bies. They report a burning sensation that began at their fingertips after prolonged petting and migrated toward the eyes over days. This pathway, if confirmed, would make the disease far more contagious than currently believed.

Pathway 03

The Surgical Pathway

Every veterinarian on the island contracted Eye-bies. Full PPE — gloves, masks, goggles — provided no protection. Two vets who performed internal surgeries infected within 48 hours. One who only performed external exams took 3 weeks. The implication: the bacteria may pass through mucous membranes under surgical conditions, by a mechanism not yet understood.

— Island survivor, North American coast

The Crossing

"By day four at sea, someone said the sky looked different. We all knew what that meant. Nobody said the word. By day twelve there were arguments — some people wanted to turn back for the dogs. Three people went overboard in a storm. We stopped counting the days after that. We just watched the water go gray."

— Island survivor, North American coast

The Dogs

Unknowing Carriers

The dogs show no symptoms. No behavioral changes. No physical signs. They remain affectionate, healthy, and — to their owners — completely normal. This is perhaps the cruelest aspect of Eye-bies: the source of the pandemic is also its most innocent victim. Several animal rights groups have argued that the dogs are not responsible and should not be harmed.

Dog Owners

Love and Risk

Dog owners are at the center of the ethical storm. Many infected individuals on the island knew the risk and continued daily contact with their dogs anyway. Public health officials have described this as irrational. Dog owners describe it as love. The debate over whether this choice should be legally restricted is ongoing.

Victims

Life in Gray

Those who have lost their color vision describe the experience as disorienting but not debilitating. "The world still works. It's just… flat," said one survivor. Others report severe psychological effects — depression, disconnection, grief. An antidote search is underway but unfunded at scale. Victims have begun organizing to demand research money.

Veterinarians

Unprotectable

Every veterinarian on the island contracted Eye-bies. The profession is now grappling with an impossible situation: they are trained to help animals, but helping infected dogs puts them at guaranteed risk of infection. Several vet associations have issued guidance recommending full withdrawal of care from unquarantined dogs in affected zones.

Activists

A Movement Divided

Animal rights activists are caught between their commitment to protecting dogs and the reality that dogs are the vector of the disease. Some are fighting to prevent culling. Others have split from their movements to support quarantine. The hashtag #SaveTheDogs and #SaveOurSight trend simultaneously — sometimes from the same accounts.

News Reporters

Signal and Noise

Media coverage of Eye-bies has been polarized from the start. Some outlets run sympathetic dog-owner profiles. Others run fear-driven outbreak maps. Misinformation — including claims that the disease is airborne, or that it affects cats too — has spread faster than accurate reporting. Three journalists covering the story have themselves contracted the disease.

Theory 01

The Saliva Theory

Supported by 6 of 9 survivors. Proponents argue that the bacteria are fragile outside the oral environment and cannot survive transmission through skin. If true, this means casual proximity to dogs is safe — only direct oral-to-facial contact carries risk. This theory, if confirmed, would significantly narrow the scope of required quarantine measures.

Theory 02

The Full Contact Theory

Supported by 2 of 9 survivors — but those 2 cases are unexplained by Theory 01. Proponents argue the bacteria can penetrate through skin, particularly in areas of high nerve density. If true, any physical contact with an infected dog is dangerous. This theory, if confirmed, would make Eye-bies one of the most contagious diseases ever recorded.

The Owner Who Refused

— Island resident, Week 2 account

Case File 03

The Crossing

"We started with 140. By the end of week one at sea, 30 people were too weak to stand. The hardest part wasn't the hunger or the waves. It was watching the sunsets go gray. Every day a little less color. By the time we saw the coastline, I couldn't tell if the lights were red or white. We were 9."

— Survivor #4, interviewed on arrival
