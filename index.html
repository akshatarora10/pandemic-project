Competing Theories

What People Believe

Scientists have no confirmed cause. Into that vacuum, two dominant theories have bred — and the argument between them is as contagious as the disease.

Theory 01 — Saliva Mutation
The bacteria mutated specifically in dogs' saliva. Only direct oral contact near the eyes can transmit the disease. Casual proximity is safe.
Theory 02 — Full Contact Spread
Any bodily contact can transmit the bacteria. There is no safe distance. All physical contact with infected dogs is dangerous.

A Diary from the Inside

A 19-year-old dog owner documented her experience with Eye-bies in real time — from the first symptom to hiding her dog from inspectors. These are her words.

Monday · 3/30/26 · Mood: Worried

It started as a mere soreness in my eyes... This morning before I left for my classes, I went to clean and fill up Cookie's water bowl but for some reason her hot pink collar looked...lighter?...like the color was faded. I've heard many instances of this happening on the news with dog owners, but I had been extra careful and didn't think it would happen to me.

Wednesday · 4/1/26 · Mood: Relieved

I woke up today to see my pink vibrant walls in a dull and lifeless slate. It was all gray. My colorful nails, the flowers on my nightstand that I had received for my 19th birthday, and...my pictures that contained all my memories.

There was a knock on my door — an inspection to take dogs away if the owner had obtained Eye-bies...and bruh, I was highkey cooked. I hid Cookie in my ottoman and told them I haven't had a dog in so many years. They knew I was lying. Luckily, I hid her well so they said they will come back in a month.

Friday · 4/3/26 · Mood: Bittersweet

I saw a rainbow today...or should I say, an arch of grayness painted in the perished sky, as I was jogging in the park. Everyone was taking pictures and talking about its beauty. After some time, you kinda forget what color looks like. But it's all good. I love Cookie and if I have to live like this to make sure she stays with me then I will do all that it takes.

"Everything went gray."

What Is It

Eye-bies is a fictional disease that creates a profound ethical dilemma: people must decide what kind of relationship they will have with dogs when that relationship costs them their ability to see color. Rooted in present-day tensions around pet ownership, public health, and animal rights.

The Condition

Infected individuals progressively lose color vision — a condition called achromatopsia. Colors simply become less saturated over weeks, then months, until the world is entirely gray. There is no known reversal. No known cure.

Origin

The disease emerged from a bacterial mutation in dogs on a remote Atlantic island. The mutation was invisible — no symptoms in the animals, no warning to the humans. By the time the first cases were understood, the infection had spread across the entire island community.

Current Status

The island has been quarantined. Survivors who escaped to North America continue to tell their stories. Scientists disagree about the transmission mechanism. Activists and governments remain at odds. The antidote search is underfunded and ongoing.

Pet Owners

Loyalty Over Sight

Even after understanding the risk, the majority of dog owners chose to keep their animals rather than seek treatment. Many describe the choice as simple — "It's just color. My dog is my family." Public health officials have described this as the central obstacle to containment.

Classified
Society

A Community Divided

On the island, neighbors stopped speaking. Dog owners were shunned — or sheltered. Protests broke out over forced quarantine orders. The social fractures of Eye-bies mapped almost perfectly onto existing political divisions: individual rights versus collective safety.

Classified
No Consensus

No Agreed Response

Three separate international health bodies have issued conflicting recommendations. One calls for immediate culling of dogs in affected areas. Another argues for quarantine and testing. A third says the data is insufficient for any policy. Meanwhile, no antidote research is funded because no government can agree on the mechanism of spread.

Classified
Pathway 01

The Water Bowl Pathway

The leading hypothesis. Bacteria multiply in the water bowls of infected dogs. When a dog drinks, the bacteria colonize the saliva. Face licking — especially near the eyes — deposits the bacteria directly into one of the body's most vulnerable entry points. Infection follows within 10–14 days. Supported by 6 of the 9 surviving islanders.

Classified
Pathway 02

The Contact Pathway

Two survivors described never allowing their dogs to lick their faces — yet still contracted Eye-bies. They report a burning sensation that began at their fingertips after prolonged petting and migrated toward the eyes over days. If confirmed, this would make the disease far more contagious than currently believed.

Classified
Pathway 03

The Surgical Pathway

Every veterinarian on the island contracted Eye-bies. Full PPE — gloves, masks, goggles — provided no protection. Two vets who performed internal surgeries infected within 48 hours. The implication: the bacteria may pass through mucous membranes under surgical conditions, by a mechanism not yet understood.

Classified
The Dogs

Unknowing Carriers

The dogs show no symptoms. No behavioral changes. No physical signs. They remain affectionate, healthy, and — to their owners — completely normal. This is perhaps the cruelest aspect of Eye-bies: the source of the pandemic is also its most innocent victim.

Classified
Dog Owners

Love and Risk

Many infected individuals on the island knew the risk and continued daily contact with their dogs anyway. Public health officials have described this as irrational. Dog owners describe it as love. The debate over whether this choice should be legally restricted is ongoing.

Classified
Victims

Life in Gray

Those who have lost color vision describe the experience as disorienting but not debilitating. "The world still works. It's just flat," said one survivor. Others report severe depression, disconnection, grief. An antidote search is underway but underfunded. Victims are organizing to demand research money.

Classified
Veterinarians

Unprotectable

Every veterinarian on the island contracted Eye-bies. The profession is now grappling with an impossible situation: they are trained to help animals, but helping infected dogs puts them at guaranteed risk of infection. Several vet associations have issued guidance recommending full withdrawal of care from dogs in affected zones.

Classified
Activists

A Movement Divided

Animal rights activists are caught between their commitment to protecting dogs and the reality that dogs are the vector. Some are fighting to prevent culling. Others have split from their movements to support quarantine. The hashtag #SaveTheDogs and #SaveOurSight trend simultaneously — sometimes from the same accounts.

Classified
News Reporters

Signal and Noise

Media coverage has been polarized from the start. Some outlets run sympathetic dog-owner profiles. Others run fear-driven outbreak maps. Misinformation — including claims that the disease is airborne — has spread faster than accurate reporting. Three journalists covering the story have themselves contracted the disease.

Unverified
Theory 01

The Saliva Theory

Supported by 6 of 9 survivors. Proponents argue the bacteria are fragile outside the oral environment and cannot survive transmission through skin. If true, casual proximity to dogs is safe — only direct oral-to-facial contact carries risk. This would significantly narrow the scope of required quarantine measures.

Unverified
Theory 02

The Full Contact Theory

Supported by 2 of 9 survivors — but those 2 cases are unexplained by Theory 01. Proponents argue the bacteria can penetrate through skin, particularly in areas of high nerve density. If true, any physical contact with an infected dog is dangerous — making Eye-bies one of the most contagious diseases ever recorded.
